Cooling


Cooling System Components

These are some parts that help control a computer’s temperature:


    Case Fans: Case fans help move air through the computer.
  • Intake Fans (in the front) bring cool air inside.


  • Outtake Fans (in the back and top) push warm air out.

  • Some cases also have fans on the side
  • Fan Filters help keep dust and dirt out.

Power Supply: The ATX power supply helps cool the computer by pushing hot air out the back.

Heat Sink Heat sinks are made from materials like aluminum or copper and help cool down parts of the computer.

  • Active Heat Sinks: Have a fan to cool parts faster (used for CPUs, high-end video cards, and some chipsets).

  • Passive Heat Sinks: Do not have a fan, but they rely on the air around them to cool down parts (used for low-end video cards and memory).

  • Active heat sinks are better at cooling than passive ones.

    • Liquid Cooling: Used when air cooling isn’t enough.
  • It includes tubes, cooling plates, a reservoir, and a radiator.


  • Liquid cooling works faster than air cooling, which is why it’s used for high-performance systems like gaming computers.

      • Uses separate connections for each slot, making it faster and more efficient.

      Much faster than PCI, with speeds depending on the version:

    • PCIe 1.0: 250 MBps (x1) to 4 GBps (x16)
    • PCIe 2.0: 500 MBps (x1) to 8 GBps (x16)
    • PCIe 3.0: 1 GBps (x1) to 16 GBps (x16)
    • PCIe 4.0: 2 GBps (x1) to 32 GBps (x16)

      • x1 slots are often for network and sound cards, and x16 slots are for video cards.
      PCIe cards fit into larger slots, but not the other way around

    Legacy Buses

      These are old bus types that are not used much anymore:
    • AGP (Accelerated Graphics Port): Used for video cards.

    • AMR (Audio/Modem Riser): Adds extra cards like audio or modem cards.

    • CNR (Communications Network Riser): Adds network, sound, or modem functions.

    Video Cards and Capture Cards

    Video cards process graphical information to display on a monitor. Their quality and speed have improved significantly, especially with PC gaming and cryptocurrency mining. A capture card is also becoming popular for streaming and recording games or videos.

    Integrated vs. Dedicated Video Cards


    Video cards can either be integrated into other components or installed as dedicated expansion cards.

    Integrated Video Cards:

    • The graphics processing unit (GPU) is built into the CPU or motherboard.

    • Shares system memory for graphics.

    • Cheaper but less powerful.

    • Common in laptops, pre-built PCs, and servers.

    Dedicated Video Cards:

    • Installed in an expansion slot on the motherboard.

    • Has its own GPU and dedicated memory.

    • Used in high-performance tasks like gaming or cryptocurrency mining.

    • Can link multiple cards together (called daisy chaining).

    Characteristics of Video Card

    When choosing a video card, consider the following:

      Display Connectors:

    • IVideo cards use connectors like DisplayPort, HDMI, USB-C, and DVI to connect to monitors.

    • Some cards support multiple monitors (2 to 6).

    • Special cables (e.g., USB-C to HDMI) may be needed.

      Display Quality:

    • Resolution: Higher resolution shows more detail (e.g., 1920x1080 or 4096x2160).

    • Refresh Rate: The number of times the image updates per second (measured in Hz). A higher refresh rate (75 Hz - 85 Hz) is better for smooth performance.

      • Processing Capabilities:

    • The GPU is better at handling graphics than the CPU.

    • Video hardware acceleration uses the GPU to speed up graphics rendering.

    • Overclocking can increase the GPU's speed but may cause more heat and wear.

      • Memory:

    • Dedicated video cards use high-speed memory (from 1 to 24 GB).

    • Types include DDR3, DDR4, DDR5, and GDDR memory (which is specifically for graphics).

      • Bus Type:

    • Video cards connect to the motherboard using PCIe x16 slots, which are the most common.

    • Older types like AGP are outdated now.

      • Multi-GPUs:

    • You can link multiple video cards together to share the workload.

    • SLI (NVIDIA) and CrossFire (AMD) are common technologies for this.

      • HDMI Audio:

    • HDMI cables can carry both video and audio signals. Some video cards allow for both to be transmitted.

      • DirectX/OpenGL:

    • DirectXand OpenGL are standards that improve graphics and multimedia. Make sure your video card supports the correct version for the best performance.

      • HDCP:

    • HDCP protects digital media from copying. If you want to watch protected content, ensure your video card supports HDCP.
