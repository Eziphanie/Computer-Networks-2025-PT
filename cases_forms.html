CASES AND FORM FACTORS


Motherboard Form Factor Standards

Motherboard form factors are design specifications that ensure computer components are compatible across different brands and technologies.
Understanding motherboard form factors is essential for customizing and upgrading your system.


What are Motherboard Form Factors?

A motherboard's form factor determines its physical features, including:

  • Dimensions
  • Power supply type
  • Mounting hole locations
  • Expansion slot count
  • Back panel layout

Common Motherboard Form Factors:

ATX (Advanced Technology Extended)
The most widely used form factor, with several versions offering varying sizes and features.

Standard ATX: 12" × 9.6" with up to seven expansion slots.

Extended ATX (EATX): 12" × 13" with more space for memory slots.

MicroATX: 9.6" × 9.6" with fewer expansion slots (four).

ITX (Information Technology Extended) Designed for small, low-power systems like home theater PCs.

Mini-ITX: 6.7" × 6.7", one expansion slot.

Smaller ITX versions include Nano-ITX, Pico-ITX, and Mobile-ITX.

BTX (Balanced Technology Extended)
A design for improved airflow, but not widely adopted.

NLX (New Low Profile Extended)
An older form factor for slimline desktops, now mostly replaced by MicroATX and Mini-ITX.

Common Computer Cases:

Computer cases are made to fit specific motherboard form factors.

Here are the most common types:

  • ATX Full-Tower: Large, spacious, compatible with ATX, EATX, and microATX.

  • ATX Mid-Tower: Smaller than full towers, fits ATX, microATX, and Mini-ITX (some).

  • icroATX Tower: Smaller, suitable for microATX and Mini-ITX.
    • m
  • Mini-ITX Tower: Designed specifically for Mini-ITX motherboards.

  • Home Theater PC (HTPC): Built to connect to TVs, compatible with microATX and Mini-ITX.
Notebook Cases: Vary by model, often with proprietary designs.

Computer Case Components:

When purchasing a computer case, it generally includes:
  • Case itself

  • Power supply (optional)

  • Case fans

  • Feet for the bottom of the case

  • Screws and standoffs for mounting the motherboard

  • External connectors for USB, audio, etc.
Understanding these basics will help you choose the right components for your computer build or upgrade.
